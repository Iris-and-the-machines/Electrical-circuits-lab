Chapter 13 Experiments

13.1 Getting used to the oscilloscope

First, familiarize yourself with the oscilloscope. To do so, you will measure the output of the function generator.

• Connect your breadboard to the function generator: Connect the cable to the silver connector (this is a BNC connector) labelled Output 50 Ohm and connect the two clamps to two wires, one connected to the + line of the breadboard and one connected to the - line of the breadboard. The convention is to use red for the + line and black for the - line. Switch on your function generator and push the button with a sinusoidal wave symbol.

• Connect the oscilloscope: Turn on the oscilloscope, wait for it to boot, press Default Setup. This sets the oscilloscope to predefined starting settings. Connect two other wires to the + and - lines and connect them to the oscilloscope probes. The probes should be connected to be inserted in Positions 1 and 2 (yellow and green marks). Remember to take off the rubber caps of the oscilloscope probes!

• Press Auto Scale on the oscilloscope. You should see two curves, indicated in yellow and green. What is their shape? Sketch them in your lab book. The knobs with the up-down arrows allow you to shift the position of the waves up and down on your screen. The knob with the left-right arrow allows you to shift the waves left and right. On the top left corner of the screen, you can read the scale (in voltage) of the two outputs from 1 and 2. Adjust it with the knobs placed just above the oscilloscope probes, so that the two waves have the same scale.

• Change the frequency of your wave with the frequency knob on your function generator. Can you observe a change in the oscilloscope signal? Measure the period of the wave, reding of the distance between two maxima of the wave. In the top part of your oscilloscope screen there is the scale bar you should use for the x-axis, it is in microseconds. You can adjust it with the left knob in the area of the oscilloscope called Horizontal.

• Change the amplitude of the wave with the amplitude knob on the function generator. Adjust the amplitude to 20V. Observe the change in signal and measure the wave amplitude on the oscilloscope.

13.2 Build a simple electrical circuit

Now, add a resistor to the system:

• Connect a 10 kOhm resistor. Make sure, the resistor closes the circuit or in other words, connects the + and the - lines.

Hint 1: You have to use additional wires for this.

Hint 2: Make sure the two “feet” of the resistors are not connected to each other through the invisible breadboard connections.

• Then, connect the function generator to the + and - lines again. This time set it to square waves and set the frequency to 0. This will produce a constance current (DC) like in the simulation exercise last time.

• Measure the voltage at the + and - lines like you did before, and read off the amplitude (i.e. voltage). The amplitude should still be 20V.

• Switch off the function generator. It is not neccessary, but safer to switch off or disconnect the generator while changing the circuit.

13.3 Resistors in series

• Extend your circuit to two 10 kOhm resistors in series. For more complicated circuits, it is convenient to use both sides of the breadboard, so that the + is on one side and the - on the other side.

• Use one oscilloscope probe to measure the voltage across the whole circuit. The result should be 20V. Use second probe to measure the voltage after the first resistor.

• What do you measure? Is it what you expect? Enter your answer in the e-test (Test question 2)

• Now change one of the resistors to 1 KOhm (10 times smaller) and measure the voltage across the whole circuit and across each resistor. Which voltage do you measure for the whole circuit, after the 10 kOhm resistor and after the 100 kOhm resistor? (Test question 3). Verify your results by calculating the values using Ohm’s law.

13.4 Resistors in parallel

• Build an electrical circuit with two 10 kOhm resistors in parallel, just as you did in the virtual experiment. Use the oscilloscope to measure the voltage across each resistor. Is it different or is it the same? (Test question 4)

• Replace one of the two resistors in parallel with a 100 kOhm resistor (the other is still 10 kOhm). Use the oscilloscope to measure the voltage across each resistor. Is it different or is it the same? (Test question 5)

13.5 Measure and unknown resistor

• In your box with the electrical parts, you will find one resistor wrapped in tape. Place it in a circuit in series with a 100 kOhm resistor. Use what you have learned to figure out it’s resistance. What is the value of your “mystery resistor”? (Test question 6)

13.6 Bonus exercise: tuning the brightness of an LED

The colored LEDs become brighter when current goes through them. The higher the current, the brighter the LED.

• Make a simple circuit where you put the LED in series with a 10 kOhm resistor.

• Adjust the amplitude of the voltage so that you can see the LED well.

• Now change the resistor to 1 kOhm and then to 220 kOhm.

• Does the LED intensity increase or decrease with increasing value of R? (Test question 6)

• Put the LED in series with two 1 kOhm resistors and pay attention to its brightness.

• Put the LED in series with the same two resistors connected in parallel. So the two resistors are in parallel, but the LED is in series. Which is brightest? Why? What does it tell you about the resistance?
